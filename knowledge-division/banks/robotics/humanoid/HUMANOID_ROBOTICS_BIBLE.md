# ATLAS Humanoid Robotics Bible

**Primary AI:** Hermes  
**Purpose:** Define the engineering foundation for safe, modular, repairable humanoid systems operating in human-built environments.

## Volume 1 — Human-Centered Robotics Engineering

Humanoids are justified when human form enables useful access to doors, stairs, elevators, tools, benches, laboratories, hospitals, factories, homes, and other spaces designed around human proportions.

Core principles:
- Function over imitation.
- Mission-first proportions.
- Modular body sections.
- Safe, predictable motion.
- Quiet operation.
- Serviceable joints and panels.
- Digital-twin traceability.

Major systems:
- Structural framework.
- Locomotion.
- Manipulation.
- Power.
- Sensors.
- Computing.
- Communication.
- Safety.
- Maintenance.

Joint architecture includes neck, shoulders, elbows, wrists, hips, knees, ankles, hands, and spine mechanisms. Every human-like feature must justify itself through function.

## Volume 2 — Locomotion and Whole-Body Movement

Movement priority:
1. Standing.
2. Weight transfer.
3. Stepping.
4. Walking.
5. Uneven terrain.
6. Stairs.
7. Ladders where justified.
8. Confined spaces.

Hermes evaluates center of mass, support polygon, ground contact, body orientation, terrain roughness, slope, friction, compliance, load distribution, step length, step width, foot angle, landing position, and recovery options.

Locomotion rules:
- Stable before fast.
- Efficient before powerful.
- Predictable before aggressive.
- Adaptable before specialized.
- Safe before impressive.

Reliability monitoring includes joint wear, bearing health, actuator performance, foot condition, fastener integrity, vibration trends, and energy consumption.

## Volume 3 — Robotic Hands, Manipulation, and Tool Use

Manipulation hierarchy:
1. Pushing and pressing.
2. Basic gripping.
3. Pick and place.
4. Tool operation.
5. Precision assembly.
6. Two-handed manipulation.
7. Delicate adaptive work.

The hand design covers finger geometry, thumb opposition, contact materials, tactile sensing, force control, slip detection, replaceable pads, modular finger assemblies, dual-arm coordination, and compatibility with existing human tools.

Performance is judged by task completion, repeatability, grip stability, safety, energy use, recovery from small errors, and maintenance time.

## Laboratory Assignments

- HR-2: Locomotion strategy across corridors, stairs, loading docks, sidewalks, uneven terrain, and light rain.
- HR-3: Manipulation system for precision assembly, electronics handling, tool use, and routine maintenance.

- HR-2: Every step is a decision.
- HR-3: A robotic hand succeeds through reliable interaction, not appearance.
