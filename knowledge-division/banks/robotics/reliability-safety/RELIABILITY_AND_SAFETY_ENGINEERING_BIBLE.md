# ATLAS Reliability and Safety Engineering Bible

## Reliability Engineering

Reliability is designed from the beginning and measured across the full lifecycle.

Core areas:
- Failure mode identification.
- Fatigue, corrosion, wear, thermal, electrical, software, sensor, and communication faults.
- MTBF and service-life estimation.
- Predictive maintenance.
- Condition monitoring.
- Redundancy where mission-critical.
- Environmental durability.
- Serviceability.
- Long-duration and accelerated testing.
- Continuous improvement from field data.

Critical monitoring signals include vibration, temperature, current draw, noise, duty cycle, backlash, calibration drift, battery health, connector performance, and communication reliability.

## Safety Engineering

Safety protects people, property, the environment, the mission, and the robot.

Hazard classes:
- Mechanical: crushing, pinch points, falling loads, sharp edges, unexpected motion.
- Electrical: shock, short circuits, damaged insulation, battery faults, overheating.
- Thermal: hot or cold surfaces, heat accumulation.
- Software: incorrect commands, state errors, communication loss, sensor faults.
- Environmental: dust, water, chemicals, slippery surfaces, poor visibility.

Safety process:
1. Identify hazards.
2. Evaluate severity, probability, and detectability.
3. Apply layered controls.
4. Define safe states.
5. Verify emergency systems.
6. Validate maintenance lockout procedures.
7. Test faults and recovery.
8. Preserve records and lessons learned.

Layered safeguards may include guards, force limits, current limits, interlocks, independent safety controllers, emergency stops, safe-speed zones, fault isolation, diagnostics, and controlled shutdown.

## Core Rules

- Reliability is measured years later, not on launch day.
- Safety is not a feature; it is the result of the entire engineering process.
- A robot should fail in a controlled and understandable manner whenever possible.
