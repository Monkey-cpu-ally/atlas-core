# Innovation Lab Prototype Gate

The Prototype Gate controls when an ATLAS project is allowed to move from idea, research, or simulation into physical testing.

The rule is simple:

> Do not build what you have not defined, scored, and made safe enough to test.

---

# 1. Purpose

The Prototype Gate protects:

- People
- Property
- Data
- Money
- Time
- The ATLAS project itself

It prevents ATLAS from jumping from exciting idea to unsafe build.

---

# 2. Required Before Prototype

A project must have:

- Project intake
- Evidence review
- Known facts list
- Assumptions list
- Unknowns list
- Risk list
- Innovation score
- Council decision
- Safety review
- Test plan
- Stop conditions

---

# 3. Prototype Levels

## Level 0 — Paper Prototype

Used for:

- UI concepts
- System diagrams
- Workflows
- Interaction design
- Project organization

No physical hazard.

## Level 1 — Digital Prototype

Used for:

- Software mockups
- CAD models
- Simulations
- Data models
- Control logic tests

No physical hazard if kept virtual.

## Level 2 — Bench Prototype

Used for:

- Small controlled electronics
- Low-power sensors
- Non-hazardous materials
- Small mechanical tests
- Educational experiments

Requires basic safety plan.

## Level 3 — Functional Prototype

Used for:

- Moving robotics
- Motors
- Batteries
- Higher loads
- Heat-producing systems
- Real sensors and controls

Requires stronger safety plan, enclosure when needed, emergency stop, and supervision.

## Level 4 — Field Prototype

Used for:

- Outdoor robotics
- Environmental devices
- Larger machines
- Long-duration testing
- Public-facing demos

Requires high caution, legal awareness, and controlled environment.

## Level 5 — Production Prototype

Used for:

- Manufacturing repeatability
- Durability testing
- User testing
- Compliance planning

Requires standards review and professional support where needed.

---

# 4. Safety Review Checklist

Before physical testing, answer:

1. What can hurt someone?
2. What can catch fire?
3. What can break under force?
4. What can leak?
5. What can overheat?
6. What can short-circuit?
7. What can move unexpectedly?
8. What can fail silently?
9. What happens if power is lost?
10. What happens if software fails?
11. What personal protective equipment is needed?
12. What tools are required?
13. What environment is safe for the test?
14. What are the stop conditions?
15. Who should supervise?

---

# 5. Stop Conditions

Every prototype test must define stop conditions.

Stop immediately if:

- Temperature rises beyond safe limit
- Smoke appears
- Electrical smell appears
- Battery swelling appears
- Unexpected motion occurs
- Structure cracks
- Fluid leaks
- Chemical reaction behaves unexpectedly
- Software loses control
- Sensor readings become impossible or unstable
- Any person feels unsafe

---

# 6. High-Risk Project Rules

The following require extra caution and may require professional support:

- High voltage systems
- Large batteries
- Combustion systems
- Hydrogen systems
- Pressure vessels
- Chemical processing
- Biological experiments
- Medical or health-related devices
- Heavy machinery
- Autonomous vehicles
- Flying systems
- Watercraft
- Robotics with strong actuators

For these, ATLAS should prefer:

- Simulation first
- Commercial certified parts
- Low-power bench tests
- Non-hazardous substitutes
- Professional review
- Standards-compliant methods

# 8. Final Rule

The first prototype should usually be smaller, safer, and simpler than the dream version.

Small tests build empires.
Careless tests burn budgets.
