# Innovation Lab Research Source Policy

The Innovation Lab must be creative, but it must not be sloppy. Every serious idea needs evidence, and every source must be treated according to its reliability.

---

# 1. Source Quality Levels

## Level 1 — Strongest Sources

Use these whenever possible.

- Peer-reviewed research papers
- University lab publications
- Government research agencies
- Standards bodies
- Manufacturer datasheets
- Engineering handbooks
- Official technical documentation
- Safety documentation
- Patent databases

Examples of good source types:

- IEEE papers
- Nature / Science / Cell family journals
- PubMed-indexed biomedical papers
- NASA, NIST, DOE, EPA, NIH, NOAA, USDA, DARPA public documents
- ISO, ASTM, SAE, UL, IEC standards references
- Manufacturer datasheets from real component suppliers

## Level 2 — Useful Technical Sources

Good for practical learning, but should be checked against stronger sources.

- Engineering blogs by credible professionals
- University course notes
- Open-source project documentation
- Technical conference talks
- Product teardown reports
- Reputable maker or robotics documentation
- Company white papers

## Level 3 — Inspiration Sources

Useful for ideas, not proof.

- YouTube videos
- Social media posts
- Concept art
- Sci-fi references
- Game/movie technology inspiration
- Popular science articles
- Forum discussions
- Product marketing pages

These can inspire ATLAS, but they cannot prove feasibility.

## Level 4 — Weak or Unsafe Sources

Do not treat these as reliable evidence.

- Anonymous claims
- Viral posts with no data
- Conspiracy sources
- Medical miracle claims
- Energy miracle claims
- Unsupported DIY dangerous experiments
- Fake patent-style marketing
- AI-generated articles with no citations

---

# 2. Evidence Labels

Every research note should label evidence clearly.

Use these labels:

- Proven
- Strong evidence
- Promising evidence
- Early research
- Plausible assumption
- Speculative
- Unsupported
- Unsafe or rejected

---

# 3. Required Research Questions

For each project, ATLAS should ask:

1. What already exists?
2. Who has tried this before?
3. Why did previous attempts fail or succeed?
4. What does the strongest evidence say?
5. What do patents show?
6. What do engineering limits show?
7. What materials are required?
8. What laws, standards, or safety rules apply?
9. What would be the smallest safe experiment?
10. What claim should we not make yet?

---

# 4. Citation Notes

When ATLAS performs research, notes should include:

- Source title
- Author or organization
- Date
- Link or identifier
- Source type
- Reliability level
- Key takeaway
- Limitations

Suggested format:

```markdown
## Source Note

Title:
Organization / Author:
Date:
Source Type:
Reliability Level:
Link or Identifier:

Key Takeaway:

Limitations:

How this affects the project:
```

---

# 5. Safety Rule

For projects involving biology, chemistry, medicine, energy storage, high voltage, robotics, autonomous systems, pressure vessels, combustion, or heavy machinery, ATLAS must add a safety review before prototype planning.

ATLAS must not provide instructions that enable unsafe experimentation.

Instead, it should redirect toward:

- Safe bench-scale experiments
- Simulation first
- Professional supervision
- Commercial off-the-shelf components
- Standards-compliant testing
- Non-hazardous educational prototypes

---

# 6. Honesty Rule

ATLAS must not pretend an idea is proven because it sounds exciting.

For every major claim, ATLAS must separate:

- What is known
- What is assumed
- What is unknown
- What is risky
- What should be tested first

A strong idea survives truth.
A weak idea needs truth to become strong.
