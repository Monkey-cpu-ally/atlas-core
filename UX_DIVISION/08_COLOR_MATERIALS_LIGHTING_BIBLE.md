# ATLAS Color, Materials & Lighting Bible

**Version:** 1.0  
**Classification:** Core Visual Standard

## Purpose

Color, materials, and lighting define the emotional atmosphere and visual hierarchy of ATLAS.

They should guide attention, communicate state, and create a timeless premium identity without reducing usability.

## Primary AI Colors

- **Ajani — Crimson:** strategy, leadership, protection, decisive action
- **Minerva — Teal:** knowledge, research, education, discovery
- **Hermes — Ivory:** engineering, manufacturing, architecture, craftsmanship
- **Council — Purple:** collaboration, consensus, shared intelligence

## Functional Colors

- Blue: neutral information and navigation
- Green: healthy systems and completion
- Amber: review, monitoring, moderate caution
- Red: critical failure, danger, and immediate attention

Functional meaning must never depend on color alone.

## Material Language

### Digital Glass

Transparent panels should preserve readability, clarify layers, and use restrained blur.

### Brushed Metal

Used to communicate engineering precision through subtle texture and controlled reflection.

### Ceramic

Used for stable, refined, professional, medical, and knowledge-oriented surfaces.

### Carbon Fiber

Reserved for robotics, manufacturing, performance, and industrial environments.

### Organic Materials

Wood, stone, fibers, and nature-inspired geometry may appear in Minerva and Hermes environments where they support the relationship between technology and nature.

## Lighting Rules

- Ambient light establishes mood.
- Task light guides attention to active work.
- AI light communicates presence and identity.
- Interactive light confirms selection and activation.
- Critical light remains unmistakable but controlled.

## Environmental Modes

- Focus Mode: reduced brightness and minimal reflection
- Learning Mode: balanced contrast and comfortable readability
- Engineering Mode: precision lighting and blueprint clarity
- Research Mode: soft illumination and reduced distraction
- Robotics Mode: stronger operational contrast and visible telemetry

## Accessibility

Every color combination must maintain sufficient contrast.

Users must be able to adjust brightness, reduce glow, disable transparency, and use high-contrast alternatives.

## Final Principle

Color, materials, and lighting should quietly guide the user.

Luxury comes from precision and restraint, not visual excess.
